What does it mean to be Dyslexic, or to have Dyslexia? Isn’t that where you read things backwards? Some common symptoms include:

  • difficulty reading behind that of peers (especially beginning in fourth grade)
  • homework taking much longer than it should
  • weekly spelling tests are studied for endlessly
  • difficulty learning to tie shoes
  • confusion of left and right, before and after, yesterday and tomorrow
  • a slow reader
  • mistake b and d when reading or writing

Each individual with dyslexia has their own mix of challenges; no two people have the exact same symptoms. Identifying if an individual has dyslexia requires several tests be administered, scored and interpreted. Children who have been tested by the school system will often receive a tag of Learning Disability or Specific Learning Disability. Small group instruction given for students with an IEP is a step in the right direction, but that instruction is often not best suited for a student with dyslexia. A dyslexic child will learn best with an individualized Orton-Gillingham approach that is simultaneously multisensory, explicit, and systematic. One-on-one tutoring with a certified tutor two to three times a week is ideal for building skills and confidence needed to get up to grade level and overcome dyslexia. There is no quick fix, but with a good support system anything is possible. Several famous people have dyslexia.
